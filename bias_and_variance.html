Bias and Variance

Under- and over-fitting

The choice of polynomial in this example is important as it relays our understanding of the physical model which gave rise to the recorded data. This understanding might be based on scientific principles such as locating earthquake epicentres using a eucliand distance metric. However, we might not know the underlying physical principle, or there might not be one, in which case we have much more freedom to specify the complexity of the model we choose to fit to our data. There are two key concepts to consider when choosing the complexity of the model to fit to the data; underfitting and overfitting.

By choosing a very simply model, such as a straight line (a 1st order polynomial), we may not be able to fully capture the variance (intracacy) in the data and the model we form might not be representative of the underlying statistical distribution of data and may not be useful for predictive purposes or for drawing conclusions about the current data. By choosing a highly complex model, such as a very high order polynomial, we risk fitting not only the underlying distribution of data but also the noise within the data, i.e. we overfit the recorded data as we fit the noise as well. This can have devasting consequences for extrapolating this model outside the range of values covered by the data used for the inversion.
